# Peer County Hybrid EMS Models — External Research
**Research date:** April 27, 2026
**Research analyst:** EMS Research Agent (external data only)
**Purpose:** Find real-world precedents for the Jefferson County ISyE 450 "regional secondary ambulance network" proposal and classify how closely each example matches the proposed model.

---

## The Jefferson Proposal (Reference Baseline)

The model being compared against has these distinctive features:

1. Municipal primaries stay independent — each municipality keeps its own primary ambulance, station, staffing, billing, and identity.
2. County/regional layer adds 3–4 strategically placed "secondary" overflow ambulances (P-Median/MCLP optimized).
3. Trigger condition: secondary unit responds ONLY when the home municipality's primary is already on a call (concurrent-call / all-busy overflow). Not first-due.
4. Dispatch: nearest-available regional unit sent via CAD/AVL integration.
5. Staffing: labor shifts from many underutilized PT secondary crews scattered across municipalities to fewer FT crews on regional units.
6. Asset reduction: fewer total ambulances countywide because secondary unit utilization rises from ~10–15% to a higher level on the consolidated regional fleet.
7. NOT a single fully consolidated county system.
8. NOT plain mutual aid (reactive and unstructured).
9. NOT a county coordinator only.
10. NOT strictly an ALS intercept (all-ability secondary transport units).

---

## 1. Executive Summary

**Key findings in five bullets:**

- No Wisconsin county currently operates a model that precisely matches all six features of the Jefferson proposal simultaneously. The Jefferson model — preserve municipal primaries + add dedicated county-level overflow-triggered transport ambulances + dispatch algorithm + staffing consolidation — appears to be a novel synthesis.
- The closest Wisconsin structural cousins are: (a) Portage County's blended contracted network with county coordinator and levy (strong on governance/funding, silent on overflow trigger), (b) Walworth County's WPF-recommended "county paramedic fly vehicle" option (county overlay unit concept, but fly vehicles are not transport-capable), (c) La Crosse County's WPF-recommended "jointly funded county-wide paramedic intercept system" (closest Wisconsin recommendation to date, described in 2020 report), and (d) the Lafayette County WPF 2025 report which explicitly recommends a paramedic fly vehicle housed at a county base as a first step toward county coordination.
- Nationally, the closest structural precedent is Erie County, NY's county ambulance program: county-purchased ambulances stationed in rural towns that function explicitly as a "safety net" and "backup when existing ambulance companies and mutual aid backups are unavailable," with local volunteer units remaining primary. Functionally near-identical to the Jefferson proposal's trigger condition.
- King County WA's Medic One is the most-cited academic cousin: a tiered ALS/BLS overlay where 27 county-operated paramedic units overlay municipal BLS fire departments. The trigger is call acuity rather than "primary unit busy," making it a structural cousin rather than a strong match.
- The Jefferson proposal's unique combination — overflow trigger, optimization-placed stations, staffing consolidation, preserved municipal identity, county-owned transport units — does NOT appear to have a complete documented precedent. The team can credibly position this as a synthesized model drawing on validated components from multiple peer systems.

## 2. Wisconsin Examples

### 2.1 Portage County, WI — Blended Contracted Network with County EMS Levy

**System name:** Portage County EMS Division (county coordinator + contracted municipal agencies)
**Location:** Portage County, WI | County seat: Stevens Point | Pop. ~70,000 | Area: ~817 sq mi

**Structure description:** Portage County does not own or directly staff ambulances. Instead, the county sheriff's office EMS Division manages and oversees a countywide EMS system. The county contracts with three municipal fire departments — Stevens Point FD, Amherst Fire District, and Plover FD — to provide paramedic-level ambulance transport across the county. The Town of Grant independently contracts with United Emergency Medical Response. Local departments retain their identity, staffing, and operations; the county provides coordination, quality management, medical direction support, and the EMS Coordinator role. An Emergency Medical Responder (EMR) first-response network provides additional ground-level coverage. In 2025 the City of Portage added its own fire-based EMS service, demonstrating that new municipal units can join the blended network.

**Regional ambulances:** Zero county-owned ambulances. All transport capacity comes from contracted municipal units. Exact total fleet count not publicly confirmed but at minimum 4+ agencies with multiple units.

**Dispatch trigger / algorithm:** County 911 / PSAP coordinates all dispatch. No "busy primary" overflow trigger documented publicly. The system relies on geographic contracting (each contracted provider covers an assigned area) rather than a dynamic real-time overflow algorithm.

**Funding mechanism:** County EMS levy of $3,027,369 at $0.35 per $1,000 equalized value (FY2025, down from $0.36/year prior). This levy is exempt from Wisconsin's levy limit constraints under state statute. The levy funds the EMS Division's coordinator role, quality management, and the contracted service payments to municipal providers.

**Year established:** County EMS coordinator role created 2009. County-wide EMS levy has been in place for multiple years (exact founding year not confirmed, pre-2024).

**Match strength vs Jefferson proposal:** PARTIAL

**Specific differences from Jefferson proposal:**
- County does not own ambulances — contracted municipal units remain primary transport
- No dynamic overflow trigger documented — dispatch is area-assignment based, not concurrent-call triggered
- Preserved municipal identity: YES (aligns with Jefferson)
- County levy funding: YES (aligns with Jefferson)
- No explicit secondary/backup overflow ambulance layer
- Jefferson proposal adds a NEW fleet layer; Portage coordinates existing municipal fleet

**Sources:**

- https://www.co.portage.wi.gov/238/Portage-County-Ambulance-Service (ambulance service page)

### 2.2 Walworth County, WI — WPF-Recommended "County Paramedic Fly Vehicle" Option

**System name:** Walworth County Fire/EMS Study Committee (study ongoing; no county EMS system yet)
**Location:** Walworth County, WI | County seat: Elkhorn | Pop. ~105,000 | Area: ~555 sq mi

**Structure description:** Walworth County operates 15 fire/EMS departments from 18 stations, mostly volunteer or paid-on-call with some private contracts. The county commissioned the Wisconsin Policy Forum to study consolidation options; the report was released October 2025. The WPF report explicitly named a "county-financed paramedic fly vehicle to provide extra assistance as needed" as a mid-tier option between minimal cooperation and full consolidation. A fly vehicle is a quick-response vehicle (SUV/truck with paramedic equipment) that can reach a scene faster than a full ambulance to deliver ALS care — it is not ambulance/transport-capable on its own.

**Regional ambulances (proposed):** One county-financed fly vehicle (paramedic QRV) as a minimum viable option. The report also examined a regional consolidation of 7 southwest-county departments as a more ambitious option.

**Dispatch trigger / algorithm (proposed fly vehicle):** The WPF report does not specify the exact dispatch trigger for the fly vehicle in the publicly available summary. The intent appears to be intercept of high-acuity calls anywhere in the county where municipal ALS is not immediately available, rather than a pure "primary unit busy" trigger.

**Funding mechanism (proposed):** County government financed — funded through county levy as a new expenditure. No levy yet enacted.

**Year established:** Study committee active 2024–2025; no county EMS system exists yet.

**Match strength vs Jefferson proposal:** PARTIAL (fly vehicle option) / COUSIN (regional consolidation option)

**Specific differences from Jefferson proposal:**
- The fly vehicle is NOT a transport ambulance — it cannot transport patients; still requires a municipal unit for transport
- Jefferson proposal uses full ambulances that can transport, eliminating the need for a municipal secondary unit
- Trigger for fly vehicle dispatch is call acuity, not "primary unit busy"
- No staffing consolidation model described for fly vehicle
- Regional consolidation option IS a strong cousin but eliminates local municipal identity in the southwest zone
- Walworth has 15 departments vs Jefferson's ~12 EMS providers — comparable scale
- County capital contribution currently $400K/year (FY2024 confirmed)

### 2.3 La Crosse County, WI — WPF-Recommended "County-Wide Paramedic Intercept System"

**System name:** La Crosse County "Something in Common" study (2020 report; partial implementation thereafter)
**Location:** La Crosse County, WI | County seat: La Crosse | Pop. ~120,000

**Structure description:** La Crosse County commissioned the WPF to study five western county fire/EMS departments in 2020. The WPF study proposed a "jointly funded county-wide paramedic intercept system to boost EMS service levels across the county" as one of three primary options — alongside expanded mutual aid and consolidation. This is the most explicitly "overlay" option the WPF has recommended anywhere in Wisconsin: a county-funded ALS layer that operates across the county to supplement municipal BLS providers. The La Crosse FD (large career department) was envisioned as potentially playing a greater regional role. Following the report, Holmen Area FD and La Crosse FD agreed to have La Crosse's chief manage both departments — a step toward consolidation but NOT implementation of the paramedic intercept option.

**Regional ambulances (proposed):** Not specified in publicly available materials. The "intercept system" concept implies dedicated county units staffed at a county level.

**Dispatch trigger / algorithm (proposed):** Described as boosting ALS coverage across the county — implies supplemental response on high-acuity calls where local BLS lacks ALS capability, not specifically a "primary unit busy" trigger.

**Funding mechanism (proposed):** Jointly funded by member municipalities — intergovernmental cost-sharing.

**Year established:** Report published 2020; intercept system not implemented. Joint management step taken post-2020.

**Match strength vs Jefferson proposal:** PARTIAL (proposed intercept system concept) / COUSIN (actual implementation was a management merger, not intercept ambulances)

**Specific differences from Jefferson proposal:**
- WPF used "intercept" language (ALS intercept on top of BLS), whereas Jefferson proposes full ALS transport units
- La Crosse ALS intercept implies call-acuity trigger, not concurrent-call busy trigger
- Jefferson proposal would eliminate the need for separate BLS first response since secondary units are full transport capable
- La Crosse took a different path post-study (management consolidation, not intercept system)
- Jefferson has ISyE-optimized station placement; La Crosse proposal had no P-median optimization component

**Sources:**
- https://wispolicyforum.org/research/something-in-common-exploring-fire-and-ems-service-sharing-opportunities-in-the-la-crosse-county-region/ (WPF La Crosse study landing page)

### 2.4 Lafayette County, WI — WPF Paramedic Fly Vehicle + County EMS

**System name:** Lafayette County EMS (LCEMS) — county-operated system since 2021; WPF "The Next Level" study 2025
**Location:** Lafayette County, WI | Pop. ~17,000 | Area: ~632 sq mi (rural)

**Structure description:** Lafayette County is the only Wisconsin county where a collapsed municipal service forced the county to create its own EMS system from scratch. In 2020, Rural Medical Ambulance Service closed. Lafayette County government launched LCEMS in 2021 to cover the gap, using a combination full-time/paid-on-call model (originally intended to be volunteer-only but pivoted). A countywide property tax levy was imposed. However, multiple municipal EMS services still exist alongside LCEMS. The 2025 WPF study recommended options ranging from (a) a single county-housed paramedic fly vehicle in Darlington, (b) two or three fly vehicles, (c) a full countywide EMS model. This is the only WPF study to explicitly quantify and cost out a fly vehicle option as a first step toward county coordination.

**Regional ambulances:** LCEMS operates ambulances in the former Rural Medical service area. The fly vehicle option (proposed 2025) would be one to three paramedic QRVs — not transport-capable. Full county EMS option modeled using Oneida County and Marquette County as examples.

**Dispatch trigger (proposed fly vehicle):** Not fully specified in available public summaries, but implies county fly vehicle responds when local BLS needs ALS intercept — call acuity trigger, not pure "primary unit busy" trigger.

**Funding mechanism:** County EMS levy (has faced legal challenges — 2023 dispute over taxing residents whose towns have their own EMS service). WPF study 2025 commissioned to resolve this tension.

**Year established:** LCEMS created 2021. WPF study delivered February 2025. Fly vehicle options not yet implemented as of April 2026.

**Match strength vs Jefferson proposal:** PARTIAL (fly vehicle concept) / STRONG (the legal and political tensions LCEMS faced are directly instructive for Jefferson County)

**Specific differences from Jefferson proposal:**
- Lafayette County's county EMS arose from service collapse, not proactive design — Jefferson is proactively designing
- Fly vehicle is NOT a transport unit (Jefferson proposes full ambulances)
- Lafayette's levy faced political backlash from towns with their own EMS who objected to being taxed for a service they don't use — Jefferson County should note this risk
- Lafayette is much smaller (~17,000 pop) than Jefferson (~86,000) — scale difference matters
- Lafayette WPF study cites Oneida County and Marquette County as county EMS models (useful secondary benchmarks)

**Sources:**
- https://wispolicyforum.org/research/the-next-level-collaborative-strategies-to-enhance-ems-in-lafayette-county/ (WPF "The Next Level" report, Feb 2025)
- https://www.wkow.com/news/legal-battle-mounting-in-lafayette-county-over-ems-tax-imposed-to-many-without-consent/article_9eba50a4-9c5d-11ed-8509-8bec9828d61c.html (WKOW — legal challenge to county levy)

### 2.5 Washington County, WI — Resolution to Pursue Countywide EMS System (Nov 2025)

**System name:** Washington County countywide EMS system (resolution only; no system yet as of April 2026)
**Location:** Washington County, WI | County seat: West Bend | Pop. ~140,000 | Area: ~430 sq mi

**Structure description:** In November 2025, Washington County's Public Safety Committee approved a resolution allowing the county to explore creation of a countywide EMS system to "support municipal agencies." The language explicitly frames this as a supporting role, not a replacement. Chief of Public Safety Dave Seager: "This resolution is purely to identify that we are looking at the creation of a countywide system." The county board was scheduled to vote December 10, 2025. The backdrop is the West Bend–Kewaskum hybrid (6-year MOU, 2025) where West Bend FD provides paramedic transport for Kewaskum (~350 transports/year) while Kewaskum retains BLS first response. Rising operational costs, stagnant reimbursement rates, and declining volunteerism are cited as drivers.

**Regional ambulances (proposed):** Not specified in available materials. The resolution is exploratory.

**Funding mechanism (proposed):** County levy — Wis. Act 212 (2025 Wisconsin Act 212 / SB181, signed April 8, 2026) provides the levy limit exemption for regional EMS systems covering ≥232 sq mi or ≥8 municipalities.

**Year established:** Resolution passed Nov 2025; system not yet established.

**Match strength vs Jefferson proposal:** PARTIAL (same political logic; different structure not yet defined)

**Specific differences from Jefferson proposal:**
- Washington County is still in the resolution/exploration phase — no defined model
- West Bend–Kewaskum bilateral MOU is a two-party arrangement, not a regional multi-municipality overflow network
- West Bend is primary transport provider for Kewaskum (not "overflow when busy") — a service takeover, not a concurrent-call overflow model
- Washington County is larger and more urbanized than Jefferson County

### 2.6 Waushara County, WI — Fully County-Operated EMS System

**System name:** Waushara County EMS
**Location:** Waushara County, WI | Pop. ~24,000 | Area: ~630 sq mi (rural)

**Structure description:** Waushara County operates a fully county-run EMS system with four staffed stations and one volunteer station. This is a FULL REPLACEMENT model: the county owns the ambulances and directly employs (or contracts for) paramedic crews. There are no separate municipal EMS providers operating alongside the county system. Station 1 (Wautoma): 1 paramedic crew + 1 critical care paramedic crew with 2 ambulances each + 1 command/rapid response unit. Station 2 (Wild Rose): 1 critical care paramedic crew with 2 ambulances. Station 5 (Poy Sippi): 1 critical care paramedic crew with 1 ambulance. Station 6 (Hancock): 1 paramedic crew with 1 ambulance. Volunteer ambulance at Three Lakes Fire Department (BLS).

**Regional ambulances:** 7–8 ambulances across 4 stations (all county-owned/operated).

**Dispatch trigger:** Nearest available county unit responds — no "primary unit busy" concept since there is only one tier of provider.

**Funding mechanism:** County EMS levy, exempt from Wisconsin levy limits. FY2022 (payable 2023) EMS budget: $2,480,125. Levy excluded from county levy limit constraints under state statute.

**Year established:** Exact founding year not confirmed; county EMS system predates 2022.

**Match strength vs Jefferson proposal:** COUSIN (county-operated ambulances but full replacement, not overlay)

**Specific differences from Jefferson proposal:**
- Waushara is a FULL REPLACEMENT — all municipal EMS services were replaced by the county
- Jefferson proposal specifically preserves municipal primaries and adds a county layer ONLY for overflow
- Waushara has no concurrent-call overflow concept because there is one unified provider
- Jefferson is 3.5x Waushara's population — Jefferson County's scale makes full replacement much more complex
- Waushara's model may not be politically feasible in Jefferson County given Jefferson Fire Chief Wegner's quote: "We don't need another department"

**Sources:**
- https://www.co.waushara.wi.us/45098/our-ems-stations (station details)
- Tax levy information confirmed via Waushara County tax documents (via search results citing $2,480,125 FY2022 EMS budget)
- northwesternems.org: "Door, Waushara and Sawyer" counties named as using general taxing power for county-wide EMS

### 2.7 Door County, WI — Fully County-Operated Single-Provider System

**System name:** Door County Emergency Services (DCES)
**Location:** Door County, WI | Pop. ~29,000 | Area: ~482 sq mi (peninsula)

**Structure description:** Door County Emergency Services is classified as a 3rd-service municipal (county-operated) ALS provider licensed for 911 transport. The county is the sole 911 EMS transport provider across all towns, villages, cities, and islands. Four stations: Central (Sturgeon Bay) — 5 ambulances (Med 1–4, 10), 2 FT paramedic crews + 1 FT EMT crew 24/7; Sister Bay — 2 ambulances (Med 5–6), 1 FT paramedic crew + 1 peak-staffed crew; South Station — 1 ambulance (Med 7), 1 FT paramedic + 1 casual EMT/AEMT; Washington Island — 2 ambulances (Med 8–9), paid-on-call EMTs/AEMTs. Responds to ~4,100 calls/year. Hospital transport agreement with Door County Medical Center: DCMS calls county EMS for patient transport; if all crews are on 911 calls, DCMC contracts a private Green Bay company — this is an explicit concurrent-busy overflow to a tertiary provider.

**Regional ambulances:** 10 county-owned ambulances across 4 stations.

**Dispatch trigger:** County dispatch sends nearest unit — no separate municipal tier.

**Funding mechanism:** County general tax levy, exempt from levy limits (confirmed by northwesternems.org).

**Year established:** Founded as county system; history traced to the 1970s per Door County Pulse article.

**Match strength vs Jefferson proposal:** COUSIN (county ambulances, but full replacement not overlay; hospital overflow protocol is a structural cousin of busy-unit secondary dispatch)

**Specific differences from Jefferson proposal:**
- Door County is a FULL REPLACEMENT of all municipal services — no municipal primaries coexist
- The hospital overflow arrangement (call private Green Bay company when county is all-busy) is structurally analogous to the Jefferson secondary ambulance concept, but operates at the tertiary level
- Door County is a peninsula with unique geography — not easily replicable for inland Jefferson County
- Jefferson proposal does the inverse: municipalities remain primary, county units are secondary
- Door County's 4,100 calls on ~29,000 population = ~141 calls/1,000 pop — lower than Jefferson's 8,396 calls on 86,000 = ~98 calls/1,000 (by transport)

**Sources:**

- https://doorcountyknock.org/2024/12/door-county-patients-see-shorter-wait-times-for-medical-transport-as-result-of-hospital-and-county-partnership (hospital overflow arrangement)

### 2.8 Western Lakes Fire District, WI — Multi-Municipality District as Structural Template

**System name:** Western Lakes Fire District
**Location:** 11 municipalities across Waukesha and Jefferson counties, WI

**Structure description:** Western Lakes Fire District formed via consolidation of Dousman and Oconomowoc fire districts in March 2017. It provides fire and paramedic EMS to 6 full-member municipalities (Oconomowoc, Dousman, Summit, Lac La Belle, Ottawa, Merton) and contracted EMS to 4 municipalities including Jefferson County's Town of Sullivan, Village of Sullivan, Town of Ashippun, and Town of Concord. Funded by municipal contributions ($10.79 million planned for 2026 — a 4.25% increase) plus ambulance billing revenue ($3.434 million net after mandatory write-offs). Because Wisconsin levy limits constrain tax increases, a voter referendum is required to increase levy beyond the inflation cap. A November 2025 referendum failed in 5 of 7 communities, highlighting the political constraint on multi-municipality district funding.

**Regional ambulances:** Multiple ALS units at the Oconomowoc base; extended coverage via contract to Jefferson County towns.

**Dispatch trigger:** District dispatch sends closest unit from any member station — unified single-provider dispatch within the district territory.

**Funding mechanism:** Municipal levy contributions (each member municipality levies for its proportionate share) + ambulance billing. No separate "county" EMS levy. Voter referendum required for levy increases above inflation.

**Year established:** March 2017 (consolidation of two earlier districts).

**Match strength vs Jefferson proposal:** PARTIAL (multi-municipality district structure) / COUSIN (no busy-unit overflow trigger; this is a fully unified district not a primary-plus-secondary model)

**Specific differences from Jefferson proposal:**
- Western Lakes is a SINGLE UNIFIED PROVIDER across its territory — no municipal primaries coexist within the district
- Contracted municipalities (Sullivan, Ashippun) are fully served by Western Lakes — they have no separate primary ambulance
- Jefferson proposal specifically preserves municipal primaries rather than absorbing them into a district
- Referendum failure in 5 of 7 communities in 2025 shows the political fragility of multi-municipality district funding via voter referendum
- Jefferson County portion of Western Lakes district covers only ~3,000 people (Sullivan, Ashippun, Concord) vs Jefferson's full ~86,000 population

- https://www.gmtoday.com/oconomowoc_enterprise/news/western-lakes-fire-district-budget/article_7d179f43-09fc-51b1-99b9-739fcf925dfc.html (2026 budget details, 4.25% increase, $10.79M)

### 2.9 Sauk Prairie Ambulance Association, WI — Multi-Municipal Joint Commission Model

**System name:** Sauk Prairie Ambulance Association
**Location:** Sauk County + portions of 2 additional counties, WI | Service area: ~196 sq mi | Pop. served: ~14,600

**Structure description:** Founded 1969. A multi-municipal ambulance association governed by an Ambulance Commission with one representative from each member municipality (Sauk City, Prairie du Sac, Merrimac, plus multiple townships). A professional director manages day-to-day operations. Licensed at AEMT level. Municipalities jointly own and operate the association. This is a "joint third service" model: municipalities pool resources into a shared agency that is neither a municipal fire department nor a county entity.

**Regional ambulances:** Not specified in available materials.

**Dispatch trigger:** Not specified; serves a unified geographic area.

**Funding mechanism:** Municipal levy contributions from each member municipality.

**Year established:** 1969.

**Match strength vs Jefferson proposal:** COUSIN (joint municipal model; no overflow trigger; no preserved primary/secondary distinction)

**Specific differences from Jefferson proposal:**
- Joint ambulance association is the sole provider — no individual municipal primaries coexist
- No county layer — this is a purely inter-municipal arrangement
- Governance by commission (1 rep per municipality) mirrors the governance model Jefferson County could use for a shared secondary ambulance network
- Much smaller scale (~14,600 pop vs Jefferson ~86,000)
- Neutral to primary/secondary distinction — serves its whole territory uniformly

**Sources:**
- https://www.saukprairieambulance.com/ (association website)
- https://www.prairiedusac.net/emergency-services (Prairie du Sac page)

### 2.10 Dane County, WI — County ALS IGA Overlay on Municipal BLS

**System name:** Dane County EMS ALS Intergovernmental Agreement (IGA) Program
**Location:** Dane County, WI | Pop. ~560,000

**Structure description:** Dane County operates an ALS IGA program where municipal fire departments are contracted as BLS first responders and ALS providers operate countywide under a shared intergovernmental agreement. The Cottage Grove IGA is a documented example: under Wis. Stat. 66.0301, the county coordinates an ALS program with a steering committee and medical advisory subcommittee. ALS is provided by the closest available ALS ambulance regardless of jurisdictional boundaries. Providers operate under uniform Dane County EMS protocols. Dane County provides countywide dispatch (single PSAP). Sun Prairie EMS participates in this Dane County ALS IGA.

**Regional ambulances:** Not specified as county-owned; the ALS layer appears to be provided by contracted municipal or regional ALS providers (e.g., Madison FD, Sun Prairie FD) rather than county-owned fleet.

**Dispatch trigger:** Priority Medical Dispatch — ALS responds to the most appropriate call type, not specifically when a municipal unit is busy. Closest-unit dispatch across jurisdictional lines.

**Funding mechanism:** IGA cost-sharing among member municipalities; Dane County coordinates but does not appear to fund directly via a separate EMS levy.

**Year established:** IGA framework ongoing; specific founding year not confirmed.

**Match strength vs Jefferson proposal:** PARTIAL (county-coordinated dispatch; ALS overlay concept; closest-unit dispatch across boundaries)

**Specific differences from Jefferson proposal:**
- Trigger is call acuity (ALS vs BLS threshold), not concurrent-call busy
- Dane County is much larger and more urbanized — fewer coverage gaps
- Jefferson proposal adds new ambulance units; Dane model coordinates existing municipal units
- Dane's IGA uses cross-jurisdictional dispatch of existing units rather than dedicated regional overflow units
- Jefferson's optimization places units for geographic efficiency; Dane relies on closest-available from existing fixed stations

### 2.11 Milwaukee County, WI — 15-Agency Collective with County Subsidy

**System name:** Milwaukee County EMS System (collective of 15 municipal + county agencies)
**Location:** Milwaukee County, WI | Pop. ~940,000

**Structure description:** Milwaukee County operates a "collective" of 15 municipal and county agencies. Milwaukee County's Office of Emergency Management administers the system and provides a $2.5 million ALS subsidy to agencies providing advanced life support care and transport. The county 911/PSAP handles dispatch for all agencies. Municipal fire departments retain their identity and operations; the county role is administrative, financial, and dispatch-coordination. A February 2025 WPF report ("Shoulder to Shoulder") analyzed options for enhanced collaboration, including potential consolidation of mobile integrated healthcare services. The report examined Wake County NC as a comparison model.

**Regional ambulances:** No county-owned ambulances — all transport by municipal agencies.

**Dispatch trigger:** County PSAP dispatches municipal units. No documented busy-primary overflow ambulance.

**Funding mechanism:** County $2.5M annual ALS subsidy + individual municipal levies and billing.

**Year established:** County EMS administrative system long-standing; WPF report Feb 2025.

**Match strength vs Jefferson proposal:** PARTIAL (county financial support + dispatch coordination; preserves municipal identity)

**Specific differences from Jefferson proposal:**
- No county-owned or county-operated ambulances
- County role is administrative/financial support, not a service delivery layer
- Milwaukee County is 11x Jefferson's population — not a direct scale comparison
- No busy-primary overflow trigger

**Sources:**
- https://county.milwaukee.gov/EN/Office-of-Emergency-Management/EMS (Milwaukee County EMS page)

## 3. National Examples

### 3.1 Erie County, NY — County Ambulance as Backup/Safety Net (STRONGEST MATCH)

**System name:** Erie County Ambulance Program
**Location:** Erie County, NY (Buffalo metropolitan area) | Pop. ~928,000

**Structure description:** Erie County launched a county ambulance program in 2023 specifically to fill gaps in rural/southern Erie County towns where volunteer EMS availability had become unreliable. Five county ambulances (Type 3) were purchased, with the first stationed at Colden Volunteer Fire Company. Initially covering 8 towns (Aurora, Wales, Eden, Boston, Colden, Holland, Collins, North Collins); subsequently expanded to Elma and Marilla. Director Gill's explicit statement: "It's meant to be a backup when existing ambulance companies and mutual aid backups are unavailable." Additional operational description: "We keep calling it a safety net. In times of high demand, if there are resources that aren't available, as normal, then we hope to staff it." The county also deployed a fly car (intercept vehicle). The public cannot directly call the county service — it is activated through the dispatch system only. The county service explicitly does NOT displace or replace existing volunteer services.

**Regional ambulances:** 5 county-owned Type 3 ambulances + 1 fly car. Stationed in rural towns that have the highest gap risk.

**Dispatch trigger:** Activated when "existing ambulance companies and mutual aid backups are unavailable." This is functionally a concurrent-busy overflow trigger — the local volunteer unit is primary and county unit is secondary.

**Funding mechanism:** $4.67 million startup funded by county surplus; ~$2 million annual operating cost (partially offset by insurance reimbursements/billing). County-funded.

**Year established:** Launched 2023, expanded 2024.

**Match strength vs Jefferson proposal:** STRONG

**Specific differences from Jefferson proposal:**
- Erie County units are stationed AT volunteer fire stations (not at optimized P-Median locations)
- Erie County is a much larger county (928,000 pop) with a different urban/rural mix
- The trigger includes "mutual aid backups unavailable" — slightly broader than Jefferson's pure concurrent-call trigger
- Erie County has not described a dispatch algorithm or AVL/CAD optimization component
- Staffing model not fully described; likely career (county-employed paramedics/EMTs)
- Jefferson proposal explicitly includes the P-Median station optimization; Erie placed units reactively based on known gap locations

### 3.2 King County, WA — Medic One ALS/BLS Two-Tier System (STRONGEST NATIONAL COUSIN)

**System name:** King County Medic One / EMS Division
**Location:** King County, WA | Pop. ~2.3 million

**Structure description:** The Medic One/EMS system uses a two-tier model. Tier 1 (BLS): Municipal fire departments provide BLS first response using firefighter-EMTs on fire trucks and aid cars. Tier 2 (ALS): King County EMS Division operates 27 ALS paramedic units staffed by county-employed paramedics, with additional ALS units from 4 fire department agencies (Bellevue, Redmond, Shoreline, Seattle). The county EMS levy — 25 cents per $1,000 assessed value (proposed 2026–2031) — funds "virtually 100% of support for paramedic services in the regional system" plus provides partial funding to fire departments for BLS. In 2024: 255,602 firefighter/EMT responses (BLS tier); 48,258 paramedic responses (ALS tier) — ALS handles 19% of calls. Dispatcher criteria-based triage determines which tier is sent; high-priority calls get simultaneous BLS + ALS dispatch.

**Regional ambulances:** 27 county ALS units (not all transport-capable — some are fly cars/quick-response vehicles).

**Dispatch trigger:** Call acuity/triage (Priority Medical Dispatch), NOT primarily "BLS unit busy." ALS responds to all life-threatening calls, not just when BLS is unavailable. BLS may transport low-acuity patients without ALS.

**Funding mechanism:** County EMS levy (25 cents/$1,000 assessed value) — one of the most mature county EMS levies in the US. Funds county ALS fleet + BLS contributions to fire departments.

**Year established:** Medic One founding: 1968–1970 (internationally recognized as the pioneer of modern paramedic EMS).

**Match strength vs Jefferson proposal:** COUSIN (tiered county ALS overlay on municipal BLS; county levy; dispatcher routing; strong on structural concept but trigger is call acuity not busy-primary)

**Specific differences from Jefferson proposal:**
- King County ALS responds to all serious calls, not just when municipal BLS is busy — this is an ALS intercept overlay, not an overflow model
- King County has 255,602 BLS calls and only 48,258 ALS calls — ALS is the SCARCE specialized resource, reserved for acuity. In Jefferson's model, the secondary unit is a full transport ambulance, not an ALS specialty intercept.
- King County population is 27x Jefferson's — not a direct scale model
- King County's model requires highly specialized ALS-paramedics at the county level; Jefferson's secondary crews could be ALS but the trigger is availability not acuity
- Jefferson's model reduces total unit count; King County adds paramedic units on top of existing BLS fleet (increases total units)
- Jefferson's optimization uses P-Median/MCLP for placement; King County's placement is based on decades of operational experience

**Sources:**
- https://kingcounty.gov/en/dept/dph/health-safety/health-centers-programs-services/emergency-medical-services/about-us/fact-sheet (King County EMS fact sheet)
- https://cdn.kingcounty.gov/-/media/king-county/depts/dph/documents/health-safety/health-programs-services/emergency-medical-services/reports/2024-ems-annual-report.pdf (2024 annual report)

### 3.3 Pinellas County, FL — Sunstar Public Utility Model

**System name:** Sunstar Paramedics (Pinellas County EMS Authority — "public utility model")
**Location:** Pinellas County, FL (Tampa-St. Petersburg metro) | Pop. ~960,000

**Structure description:** In 1988, Pinellas County created a "public utility model" where the county owns the EMS brand and contracts all ambulance service to a private company (currently Paramedics Plus operating under the county's trade name "Sunstar"). The EMS Authority contracts with the private ambulance service for all 911 calls and non-emergency interfacility transports. Separately, 18 municipal fire departments provide ALS first response (not transport) — 62 ALS fire engines/rescue vehicles countywide. The private Sunstar transports; fire departments treat-but-don't-transport.

**Regional ambulances:** County contracts for an undisclosed number of Sunstar ambulances (sufficient to serve ~960,000 population countywide).

**Dispatch trigger:** County dispatch sends Sunstar for transport; fire is simultaneously dispatched for ALS first response. Not a "primary busy" trigger — Sunstar IS the primary transport on all calls.

**Funding mechanism:** County contracts with private provider (competitively bid every 5–10 years). Fire departments funded municipally. County EMS Authority provides oversight.

**Year established:** 1988 (public utility model founding).

**Match strength vs Jefferson proposal:** COUSIN (county-level transport provider; municipal fire for first response; but this REPLACES municipal transport, not supplements it)

**Specific differences from Jefferson proposal:**
- Sunstar is the PRIMARY transport ambulance for every call — there are no municipal transport ambulances
- Jefferson's model preserves municipal transport as primary and adds county secondary for overflow
- Pinellas model eliminated all municipal ambulance transport roles — politically much more disruptive
- Private contractor model vs county-staffed; Jefferson is considering county-employed crews
- Sunstar membership program ($99/year) provides supplemental funding — Jefferson has no equivalent

### 3.4 Pitt County, NC — County EMS Coexisting with City FD EMS

**System name:** Pitt County EMS (county system) coexisting with Greenville Fire-Rescue/EMS
**Location:** Pitt County, NC | Pop. ~169,000

**Structure description:** Pitt County EMS provides 24/7 ALS transport from 10 county stations. The City of Greenville Fire-Rescue/EMS operates 6 stations with 5 transport ambulances. Both systems coexist under unified 911 dispatch (Pitt County PSAP). County and city units are dispatched by the same communications center. This is a rare example where a county system and a municipal system genuinely coexist with separate but coordinated ambulance fleets.

**Regional ambulances:** 10 county stations + 6 Greenville city stations = 16 total EMS response points.

**Dispatch trigger:** County dispatch sends nearest appropriate unit — likely geographic assignment by service area, not strictly a "city busy — send county" protocol.

**Funding mechanism:** County tax levy (county system) + city budget (Greenville system).

**Year established:** Long-established; >20,000 annual calls.

**Match strength vs Jefferson proposal:** PARTIAL (county and municipal ambulances coexist; but county serves unincorporated areas while city serves incorporated — geographic assignment, not overflow trigger)

**Specific differences from Jefferson proposal:**
- County serves unincorporated Pitt County; Greenville serves the city — geographic partition, not overflow
- Jefferson proposal has all providers covering their own areas with the county unit serving as a backup layer anywhere in the county when a primary is busy
- No described dynamic overflow trigger between the two systems
- Pitt County units are staffed by county as primary responders in their zones, not as secondary overflow

### 3.5 Multnomah County, OR — County Franchise of Single Private Provider

**System name:** Multnomah County EMS (American Medical Response — AMR — single county franchise)
**Location:** Multnomah County, OR (Portland area) | Pop. ~830,000

**Structure description:** Multnomah County contracts all 911 ambulance transport to a single franchised provider (AMR). The county provides medical oversight, the ambulance contract, and a single EMS medical director. The county does not subsidize or directly operate ambulances. A 2023 crisis: Portland tracked 13,000+ "Level Zero" events — times when zero AMR ambulances were available to respond. The county's 2024 response was to allow a hybrid staffing model (one paramedic + one EMT instead of two paramedics). Instructive lesson: a county franchise model relying on a single private provider is highly vulnerable to staffing shortages with no backup layer.

**Regional ambulances:** AMR private fleet — county sets quantity/coverage requirements via contract.

**Dispatch trigger:** AMR is dispatched for all 911 transport calls. Fire departments provide first response. When AMR is at "Level Zero" (all units busy), there are no county backup units — a critical vulnerability.

**Funding mechanism:** AMR self-funds through billing; county provides medical oversight only (does not subsidize).

**Year established:** AMR franchise long-standing; crisis emerged 2022–2024.

**Match strength vs Jefferson proposal:** COUSIN (illustrates the vulnerability of a system with no backup layer — validates the Jefferson proposal's rationale for adding secondary units)

**Specific differences from Jefferson proposal:**
- Multnomah's crisis IS the problem Jefferson is trying to prevent — "Level Zero" is the all-units-busy scenario
- Jefferson's secondary ambulances would be the backup layer that Multnomah lacked
- Multnomah is entirely private-provider; Jefferson envisions county-operated secondary units
- Multnomah's fix was staffing ratio change; Jefferson's fix is adding dedicated secondary capacity

**Sources:**
- https://www.kgw.com/article/news/local/multnomah-county-allow-hybrid-model-ambulance-staffing-compromise-amr-late-response-time/283-aa23e321-b5c0-48f7-826d-2414cc98ccbd (KGW — hybrid model coverage)

### 3.6 Kalamazoo County, MI — County Medical Authority + Multi-Provider ALS

**System name:** Kalamazoo County EMS System (coordinated by Kalamazoo County Medical Control Authority)
**Location:** Kalamazoo County, MI | Pop. ~250,000

**Structure description:** Kalamazoo County operates a "high-performance single tier ALS system with countywide BLS first responders." Five ambulance services cover designated geographic zones; 16 first-responder agencies (fire and public safety) provide BLS first response. The county Medical Control Authority (housed within WMed/Western Michigan University Medical School) provides medical direction and system oversight. The result is ALS ambulance transport from 5 private/independent agencies with consistent county-level medical protocols and oversight.

**Regional ambulances:** 5 ambulance agencies covering geographic zones (exact fleet count not specified).

## 5. What Appears UNIQUE About the Jefferson Proposal

The following features of the Jefferson proposal do NOT appear to have a documented precedent as a combined package:

**Feature 1: Concurrent-call busy trigger for a TRANSPORT-CAPABLE secondary ambulance fleet.**
Erie County NY comes closest in language ("backup when local is unavailable"), but Erie's dispatch protocol is not fully documented. No Wisconsin county has a publicly described dispatch algorithm that sends a secondary transport ambulance specifically and only when the primary municipal unit is on a call. Walworth and Lafayette counties propose fly vehicles (non-transport), not ambulances. This specific trigger logic on a transport unit appears novel.

**Feature 2: Optimization-placed regional stations (P-Median/MCLP).**
No documented county system — Wisconsin or national — describes using operations research optimization to place secondary/overflow stations. All peer systems place units based on historical demand, geography, or existing facility locations. Jefferson's ISyE approach of running P-Median and MCLP to determine optimal secondary station placement (Fort Atkinson corridor, Jefferson city, Johnson Creek, Watertown/Ixonia corridor) is methodologically novel in the county EMS context.

**Feature 3: Labor consolidation from scattered PT secondary crews to FT regional crews as an explicit design goal.**
Peer systems justify secondary/county units on coverage grounds (geographic gaps, volunteer unavailability). None explicitly frame the model as a consolidation of underutilized PT secondary positions into fewer FT regional positions as the core staffing logic. The Jefferson proposal explicitly argues this: county secondary crews are more cost-efficient per unit of service because utilization rises from 10–15% (status quo scattered PT secondaries) to a higher level on optimized regional units.

**Feature 4: Preserved municipal primary + county secondary as a single integrated dispatch system with AVL/CAD routing.**
Some peer systems have a two-tier dispatch (King County), but it is ALS vs BLS, not primary vs secondary for the same level of care. Erie County has the right trigger logic but lacks documentation of real-time AVL/CAD integration. Jefferson's proposal envisions a fully integrated dispatch where the CAD system automatically identifies the nearest-available regional unit when the local primary is committed.

**Feature 5: Asset reduction as an explicit efficiency argument.**
The argument that adding county secondary units will allow REDUCING total ambulance count countywide (because regional units achieve higher utilization) is not documented in any peer system. Most peer systems add units; none claim a simultaneous asset reduction as a design feature.
