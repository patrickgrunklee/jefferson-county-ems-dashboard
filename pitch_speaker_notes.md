## SLIDE 3 — The IE Intervention (1:20)
*(Diagram: conceptual overlay — primaries stay, regionals overlay)*

> **"We used two industrial engineering tools that most of you know from logistics and call-center design:"**
>
> **"First — P-Median facility location. Same math Amazon uses to place distribution centers. We asked: given where the calls actually happen, where should four regional secondary stations go to minimize total patient wait time?"**
>
> **"Second — Erlang-C queuing theory. Same math that tells a bank how many tellers to staff at 11 AM. We used it to validate that four stations is enough — not two, not six."**
>
> **"The result is a hybrid system. Primaries stay local — every department keeps its first ambulance and its autonomy. But the second unit goes regional. Dispatch routes whichever ambulance reaches the patient fastest, regardless of town lines."**

**Delivery notes:**
- Name-drop P-Median and Erlang-C **once**, then translate to Amazon and bank tellers immediately. Don't dwell.
- The phrase **"every department keeps its first ambulance and its autonomy"** is the political disarm — critical. Say it clearly.
- Bridge to Slide 4: **"And here's what that actually buys us."**

## SLIDE 5 — The Bottom Line (0:25)
*(Big-numbers slide — the "Big Three" reframed for care quality)*

> **"Three outcomes — in the language that matters to a patient."**
>
> **"TIME: Roughly three minutes faster when a second call comes in. In cardiac arrest, that's the difference between a patient surviving and not."**
>
> **"QUALITY: One unified medical protocol instead of twelve. Every patient gets the same care, regardless of which side of a boundary they're on."**
>
> **"COVERAGE: The all-busy gap closes. No Jefferson County resident waits for an ambulance from another county during a medical emergency."**

**Delivery notes:**
- **Do NOT lead with dollar savings.** EMS revenue funds fire departments. Frame everything as care quality.
- If asked in Q&A about cost: *"Today the county collectively spends about $2.4 million a year on fragmented secondary capacity. The regional model is roughly cost-neutral — but it buys dramatically better coverage."*

## SLIDE 5b — The Business Case (0:25)
*(Image: `pitch_slide_5b` — 3 scenario cards anchored to Peterson's model)*

> **"And here's the business case. Today the county collectively spends about $2.4 million a year on fragmented secondary backup — across 12 independent departments."**
>
> **"Using Chief Peterson's own 24/7 ALS cost projection as our anchor, the hybrid regional model — one 24/7 station plus three peak-only stations — comes in at roughly $900 thousand net per year."**
>
> **"That's the same envelope the county already spends. But instead of 12 half-empty systems, it buys 75% coverage, 5.8 minutes faster response, and it closes the all-busy gap."**

**Delivery notes:**
- Lead with the anchor: **"Chief Peterson's own cost projection"** — this disarms skepticism because it's their number, not ours.
- Hit the three numbers: **$2.4M today → $900K hybrid regional** — same envelope, better care.
- Do NOT say "savings." Say **"same spend, better care"** or **"within the envelope the county already spends."**
- If a shark interrupts with "where's the savings?" — repeat: *"We're not selling savings. We're showing that dramatically better coverage is achievable without asking for more money."*
- Bridge to Slide 6: **"So here's what we're asking for."**

## Q&A PREP — Expected Questions

**Q: "Why not just force the departments to merge?"**
> A: "Forced consolidation isn't on the table politically, and it isn't necessary. Our model keeps every primary ambulance local — departments retain their autonomy, their revenue, and their fire service funding. We're adding a shared safety net, not taking anything away."

**Q: "Where's the ROI in dollars?"**
> A: "Today the county's 12 departments collectively spend about $2.4 million a year on distributed secondary capacity — that's the diagnostic cost of the current fragmentation. The regional model, built on Chief Peterson's own cost projection, comes in around $900K net per year for the hybrid scenario. Same envelope. The return isn't in dollars saved; it's in response-time minutes gained and coverage gaps closed."

**Q: "Walk me through the cost math — how did you get to $900K?"**
> A: "Chief Peterson built a 24/7 ALS cost model for a single station: $716K operating, $466K in EMS revenue, $250K net per station. We scaled that to 4 stations and modeled three coverage mixes. Scenario A is all four 24/7 at about $1M net. Scenario B is all peak-only at $851K. Scenario C, our recommended hybrid — one 24/7 anchor plus three peak-only — lands at $902K net with 22 FTEs. All three are below the $2.36M the county already spends on fragmented capacity."

**Q: "How do you know four stations is the right number?"**
> A: "We ran K=2 through K=6. Two stations covers 64% of demand — too thin. Six hits diminishing returns. Four is the elbow of the curve: 75% covered, 10.8 minute average response, and staffing that fits within plausible county-levy funding. We have the sensitivity analysis in the backup slides."

**Q: "What about staffing? Who pays for the paramedics?"**
> A: "Three funding scenarios model this — 24/7 ALS, peak-only BLS, and a hybrid. The hybrid scenario is $676K net operating cost with 17 FTEs, which is within the range the County Working Group has been discussing for a levy."

**Q: "What's your data source?"**
> A: "CY2024 NFIRS call records — 8,396 EMS calls across Jefferson County geography, corrected for out-of-county contamination. Cross-validated with the Chief's Association billing collections data."

**Q: "Who's implementing this?"**
> A: "That's what we're presenting to the Jefferson County EMS Working Group on April 22nd. Our deliverable is the analysis and the optimization; implementation sequencing is a county decision."

## Delivery Discipline

- **Pace:** 130 wpm feels natural; 150+ sounds rushed. Practice with a timer.
- **Pauses:** Full 1-second pause after "41 times" (Slide 1), "12 half-empty systems" (Slide 2), "from 41 to 1" (Slide 4). The pause sells it.
- **Eye contact:** Three judges, roughly left/center/right. Rotate each transition.
- **Hands:** Out of pockets. No laser pointer fidget. If the slide is doing the work, your hands can rest.
- **Ending:** Stop on "Thank you." Do not fill silence. Let them ask.
